# Objection Handling

## "We already have internal AI training."

**Response:**
Most internal AI training covers the tools — how to use ChatGPT, how to write prompts. This is different: it's a production engineering methodology. The question isn't "how do you use the tool?" — it's "how does your team ship reliable software *with* the tool, consistently, at scale?" That's a different conversation, and most internal programs don't go there.

**Follow-up question:**
> *"What does your current training cover around context architecture, specialist agent design, and production verification cycles?"*

---

## "Our developers already use AI — they'll figure it out."

**Response:**
That's exactly the pattern I see everywhere — and it's also why results are inconsistent. Some engineers get great outcomes. Most don't. And the difference is method, not talent. "Figuring it out" individually takes 6–18 months per developer and creates fragmentation across the team. One structured training session compresses that dramatically and creates a shared language.

**The cost of waiting:**
> *A team of 10 at €100k average salary spending 25% of their time on low-value AI rework = €250,000/year in lost capacity. The training costs a fraction of that.*

---

## "The AI tools are changing so fast — training will be outdated."

**Response:**
The tools change. The principles don't. Context architecture, planning before building, specialist agent design, verification cycles — these are methodology-level concepts that apply regardless of whether you're using Claude 3.5 or Claude 6. The framework is model-agnostic by design. We update the specific tooling references, but the core remains stable.

---

## "We're not ready — we're still evaluating which tools to use."

**Response:**
Interestingly, this is actually the best time to do this training. Most teams that wait until they're "set up" have already built bad habits that are harder to unlearn. The framework is tool-agnostic — it applies to Claude Code, Cursor, Copilot, or whatever you end up standardizing on. Coming in before habits are locked in is an advantage.

---

## "Can you prove the ROI?"

**Response:**
The clearest metric is engineering time recovered from AI cleanup and rework. Before the workshop, I can help you baseline: what percentage of PR review time is spent correcting AI-generated code? What's the rework rate on AI-assisted features? After 30 days, those numbers shift measurably.

More directly: teams that complete this training consistently report shipping the same features in 40–60% less time, with fewer production incidents, within 4–6 weeks of the workshop.

---

## "The price is too high."

**Response:**
Let's run the math. A half-day workshop at €3,500 for 20 engineers is €175 per person. If each engineer recovers even 2 hours per week of time currently lost to AI rework, that's €100+/week per engineer in recovered capacity. The investment pays back in weeks, not quarters.

**Reframe:**
> *"The question isn't whether €3,500 is a lot. It's whether €3,500 is a lot relative to what your team is currently losing."*
