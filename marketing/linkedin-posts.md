## Post 1 — The Method Problem (Thought Leadership)

**Hook:** Most companies have given their teams AI tools. Almost none have given them a method.

---

The gap between "we use AI" and "AI makes us faster" isn't the model.

It's the method.

I've worked with engineering teams across dozens of companies. The pattern is consistent:

→ Access to AI tools: ✅ (Copilot, Cursor, Claude, ChatGPT)
→ Consistent results: ❌

The teams that actually ship faster aren't using better models. They're using a better *system*.

Here's what separates them:

**1. Context architecture.**
They onboard the AI like a new team member — product vision, conventions, guardrails, stack. Not just a prompt.

**2. Specialist agents.**
One generalist AI for everything = mediocre at everything. Focused agents (senior-react-dev, qa-playwright-engineer, devops-pulumi-specialist) = significantly better output.

**3. Plan-first discipline.**
They never let the AI jump straight to code. Architect mode first. Always.

**4. Verification cycles.**
Trust but check. Every output. Every time.

These aren't tricks. They're a methodology.

It takes a team from ad-hoc AI experimentation to a production-grade workflow. In one day.

## Post 2 — The Context Window Is Your Org Chart (Authority)

**Hook:** The context window is your org chart.

---

Most AI agent failures aren't prompt problems.

They're architecture problems.

Think of it this way: a junior engineer who doesn't know the product vision, the tech conventions, or who to escalate to will make confident, well-intentioned, wrong decisions.

Agents are exactly the same.

What you put in the context window determines:
→ What the agent is trying to achieve
→ What constraints it must respect
→ What it can act on vs. what to escalate
→ What it doesn't know — and therefore shouldn't touch

When I started treating context design as a first-class engineering discipline, agent reliability went from "sometimes useful" to "consistently production-ready."

A few things that changed:

→ Every agent gets a scoped brief: goal, constraints, output format, escalation rules
→ Memory is curated, not dumped — distilled signal, not full logs
→ "I don't know" is a valid output. Hallucination drops when uncertainty is modeled
→ Context boundaries are explicit. Agents know where their lane ends.

The teams still debugging prompts are missing the architectural layer underneath.

Build the org chart first. Prompts are just job descriptions.

## Post 5 — Contrarian / Spiky Take

**Hook:** "AI will replace developers" is the wrong fear. Here's the right one.

---

The developers at risk aren't the ones who can't code.

They're the ones who can't direct.

AI doesn't replace engineering skill. It amplifies it — in both directions.

Engineers who can define clear context, design specialist agents, and maintain production discipline become 10x more effective.

Engineers who use AI as a fancy autocomplete, ship whatever it generates, and skip verification cycles — they get *slower*. Because they're now responsible for debugging code they didn't write and don't understand.

The skill that matters now is agentic direction:
→ Knowing how to onboard an AI to your codebase
→ Knowing when to trust the output and when to interrogate it
→ Knowing how to structure a workflow so AI makes you faster, not louder

This isn't a soft skill. It's a methodology.

And like any methodology, it can be taught.
